Parking Meter

Product & UI Design

Public parking meters tend to be atrocious, both aesthetically and intuitively. I want to design a beautiful, more usable parking meter that also embodies the neighborhood of North Shore in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania.

Developing Concepts

Usability Testing

To better understand the size and shape of my chosen design, I created a low fidelity prototype with adjustable components to test on multiple users. I had users move the touchscreen to their preferred height both while standing and sitting to accommodate potential users in a wheel chair. Working in 3-dimension also allowed me to gain insights regarding the infeasibility of a physically dynamic touchscreen and placement of the static payment interface.

Human Factors Analysis

Interface components are designed to accommodate a 5th percentile female in a wheelchair, a 95th percentile standing male, and everyone in between.

Formal Exploration

Interface Design & Testing

I created a wireframe of the interface to test the flow on various users in a rapid and informal way. I also wanted to delve deeper into the method of selecting parking time, as I was particularly frustrated with the existing solution. I explored more visual representations of time selection that were more intuitive and relied on a playful analogy of the sun rising and setting.

Interface Flow

Final Product

With a shape inspired by the iconic entryway to North Shore, this parking meter uses a solar panel to power the virtually dynamic interface, accommodating users of all sizes.
